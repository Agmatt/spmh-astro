There are moments in the life of an institution that deserve to be remembered. Not just noted in a report or filed in an archive, but genuinely remembered — the kind of moment you recount years later to a colleague who wasn't there, saying, *"You should have seen it."* The 30th of July 2025 was one of those moments for St. Paul's Mission Hospital.

On a Thursday morning in Homabay, something that had existed for years only in plans, conversations, and the quiet hopes of those who run this hospital finally met the earth. The groundbreaking ceremony for the new Surgical and Maternity Wing had arrived.

---

### It Began at the Altar

Before a single photograph was taken or a single speech delivered, the day began the way significant days at this hospital tend to begin — in prayer.

A morning Mass was celebrated by **Father Romanus** and **Father Duncan**, gathering the hospital community and guests before the formalities of the day commenced. It was a grounding moment in every sense of the word. For a Catholic mission hospital whose identity is inseparable from its faith, beginning a project of this magnitude at the altar was not ceremonial — it was foundational. The work that would follow was being placed, from the very first hour, under something larger than architectural drawings or construction timelines.

The congregation that morning reflected the breadth of what this project represented. Board members were present. Architects and designers — the people who had translated a vision into blueprints — sat alongside masons who would eventually translate those blueprints into walls. Representatives from the Diocese of Homabay filled the space alongside hospital staff and members of the wider community. Everyone who had a stake in what was about to happen had found their way to that Mass.

---

### The Bishop Arrives

The main guest of the day was **His Lordship Bishop Michael Odiwa**, who heads the Catholic Diocese of Homabay — the diocesan authority under which St. Paul's Mission Hospital operates. His presence elevated the occasion, but what struck those in attendance was not the weight of his office. It was what he said when he spoke.

Bishop Odiwa congratulated the staff of SPMH — not for the groundbreaking itself, but for something older and quieter: the compassionate care they extend to patients every single day. He acknowledged the humanity in that work, the choice it requires, and the dignity it gives to people who arrive at this hospital in their most vulnerable moments. His words carried the kind of recognition that staff rarely hear articulated so directly, and the room received them with visible appreciation.

He then turned to the project at hand. Good infrastructure, he said, is not a luxury — it is the backbone of confident service delivery. A health worker equipped with the right environment, the right tools, and the right space does not just work better. They believe more in what they are doing, and that belief travels. It reaches the patient on the bed. It shows in the outcome.

He closed his remarks with a call that felt both personal and institutional — an encouragement to every person present, and by extension every member of the hospital community, to take active ownership of the success of their place of work. Not as employees fulfilling a contract, but as people with a genuine stake in what this hospital becomes.

---

### The Ground Breaks

From the hall, the gathering moved to the site itself — the piece of ground that would, in time, become the new Surgical and Maternity Wing.

Bishop Odiwa led the proceedings at the site, offering prayers over the ground and conducting the cleansing ceremony that preceded the break. There was a stillness in that moment that people who were present will remember. The site was open sky, bare earth, and possibility — and the prayers said over it felt entirely appropriate to what was being asked of it.

Then came the moment the cameras had been waiting for.

The Bishop climbed aboard the excavator and, to the delight of everyone gathered, made the first break in the ground himself. The machine moved, the earth gave way, and something that had lived in vision and planning crossed over into physical reality. The cheer that went up from the crowd was not just for the Bishop or for the machinery — it was for everything the moment represented. Years of advocacy, months of preparation, and the quiet persistence of a hospital administration that refused to stop pushing for better.

The ground had broken. The work had begun.

---

### A Gallery of Smiles

What followed was, by all accounts, one of the more joyful parts of the morning. The formal proceedings gave way to photographs — group photographs, candid photographs, photographs of the Bishop with staff, of architects with management, of board members with the people who clean the wards and prepare the theatre. The hierarchy of the morning dissolved into something more human, and the camera caught it all.

These photographs will matter more than anyone present that day might have imagined. In three years, or five, when the new wing is receiving patients, and a nurse walks its corridor for the first time on a night shift, someone will pull up a photograph from the 20th of July 2025 and say — *This is where it started. This is the day we broke the ground.*

---

### What the New Wing Will Mean

The significance of a new Surgical and Maternity Wing at SPMH cannot be overstated for the community it serves. Surgery and maternity care sit at two of the most consequential intersections of human life — the repair of the body, and the arrival of new life into the world. The conditions in which those things happen matter enormously, both for outcomes and for dignity.

For a hospital that has been steadily growing its service capacity — adding a Newborn Unit, developing an HDU, achieving Level 4 accreditation — a dedicated surgical and maternity wing is the next logical and necessary step. It will expand the hospital's ability to manage complex deliveries, support a wider range of surgical cases, and provide the kind of environment that patients deserve and that clinical staff need to do their best work.

For the mothers of Homabay, in particular, it will mean something very simple and very profound: a place built for them.

---

### To Everyone Who Made the Day Possible

A groundbreaking ceremony is, at its core, a community act. It takes board members willing to approve and support a vision, architects willing to design it, engineers and masons willing to build it, administrators willing to fight for it, and a diocese willing to back it. It takes a Bishop willing to climb an excavator on a Sunday morning and put his hands to the work.

All of those people were on that site on 30th July 2025. And the hospital — and the community it serves — is better for every single one of them.

The ground has been broken. The future of St. Paul's Mission Hospital is under construction.

*Safe Hands, Caring Hearts.*
